Spite - Ashes of Ephtael

Spite is a Diablo-inspired action RPG developed in a team environment using our in-house engine, Lamp Engine. The project focused on combat readability, enemy behaviour and responsive top-down gameplay.

Overview

Spite was built around fast-paced combat inspired by Diablo-style gameplay. A key focus was making enemy behaviour readable and responsive, ensuring the player could understand and react to threats during combat.

My work was primarily focused on enemy systems, where I designed and implemented behaviour for a ranged enemy type that pressures the player from a distance using projectile attacks. This required structuring behaviour logic, integrating animation and ensuring the enemy fit naturally into the combat flow.

Systems & Features

Enemy AI & State Machine

Enemy behaviour was implemented using a state machine to clearly define transitions between states such as idle, chase and attack. This approach made behaviour predictable, extendable and easier to debug within the combat system.

Ranged Enemy Behaviour

I developed a ranged enemy archetype that attacks the player using projectile-based mechanics. The goal was to create pressure at a distance while maintaining clear and readable attack patterns.

Animation Integration

Integrated enemy animations with gameplay logic to ensure actions such as attacks and movement aligned visually with behaviour states. This was important for readability and player feedback.

Camera & Input Systems

Contributed to systems such as CameraManager and InputManager, helping ensure responsive controls and consistent camera behaviour during gameplay.

Technical Takeaways

This project gave me practical experience working inside a custom engine and building gameplay systems from the ground up. I gained a deeper understanding of how AI, animation and input systems interact to create a cohesive gameplay experience.

Working on enemy behaviour in a combat-heavy game highlighted the importance of timing, readability and structure. Even small design decisions in behaviour logic had a direct impact on how the game felt to play.
